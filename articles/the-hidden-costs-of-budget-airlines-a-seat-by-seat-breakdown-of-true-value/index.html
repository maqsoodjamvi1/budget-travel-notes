The allure of a $39 flight across the country is hard to ignore. When you see that price on a search engine, your brain immediately does the math: a round-trip ticket for the price of a nice dinner out. But after flying budget carriers like Spirit, Frontier, and Allegiant for years, I’ve learned that the sticker price is just a suggestion.

If you don’t play by their rules, that $39 ticket can quickly balloon to $200. Here is how the actual costs break down and how you can decide if the savings are worth the trade-offs.

The Carry-On Trap

On legacy carriers like Delta or United, a carry-on bag is usually included in your standard economy fare. On budget airlines, it is almost never free. In fact, it is often the most expensive add-on.

When you book, you’ll see the “base fare.” The moment you select a carry-on, the price jumps by $50 to $70 each way. If you are traveling as a couple, that’s an extra $200 round-trip just for the privilege of overhead bin space.

My strategy: I invested in a “personal item” backpack that meets the specific dimensions allowed by these airlines (usually 18x14x8 inches). If I can fit three days of clothes into that, I pay zero for luggage. If you cannot live without a roll-aboard bag, calculate the $120 total round-trip baggage fee into your flight search before you compare it to a major airline. Often, the “expensive” legacy ticket is actually cheaper once you factor in the included bag.

Seat Selection as a Hidden Fee

Budget airlines operate on an “unbundled” model. They sell you the right to sit in a metal tube, but they don’t promise you a specific seat. If you are flying with a partner or child and want to sit together, you will pay a “seat selection fee.”

These fees usually range from $15 to $45 per segment, depending on how close you are to the front of the plane. If you decide to skip the fee and take your chances, the computer will likely put you in a middle seat toward the back. On a 4-hour flight, that might be fine for a solo traveler, but for families, it is a logistical headache that usually results in you paying the fee at the last minute anyway.

The “Gate Agent” Tax

This is where most first-time budget travelers get burned. If you show up at the gate with a bag that is slightly larger than the sizer bin, or if you failed to print your boarding pass (and the airline charges for printing), the costs are astronomical.

These fees are not set at “cost-recovery” levels; they are punitive. I once watched a passenger get charged $99 at the gate because their duffel bag wouldn’t slide into the metal sizer. If you are flying these airlines, you must be your own gate agent. Use the airline’s app to check in, download your mobile boarding pass, and measure your bag at home before you leave. If you are even an inch over the limit, check it online in advance. Paying $45 to check a bag online is better than paying $99 at the gate.

Comfort vs. Time

Budget airlines often fly into secondary airports. For example, you might land at an airport that is 45 minutes away from your actual destination city.

Before booking, calculate the cost of getting from that airport to your hotel. If a flight to a major hub costs $50 more but saves you a $60 Uber ride or two hours on a regional bus, you are actually losing money by taking the “cheaper” flight. Always pull up Google Maps and check the driving distance and transit options from the arrival airport to your final destination before hitting “confirm.”

When Budget Airlines Actually Make Sense

Despite the fees, these airlines serve a purpose. They are excellent if:

  • You are a solo traveler: Sitting in a middle seat for a two-hour flight is manageable.
  • You travel light: If you can master the “under-seat” personal item, you beat their business model.
  • The flight is a “true” budget route: If a legacy carrier wants $400 for a flight and the budget carrier is $80—even with a $50 bag fee—you are still coming out ahead.

The math you should do before every booking:

  1. Take the budget airline’s base fare.
  2. Add the cost of a round-trip carry-on bag.
  3. Add the cost of a seat assignment (if you are traveling with others).
  4. Add the cost of ground transportation from the secondary airport.
  5. Compare that “True Cost” total against the base fare of a legacy airline that flies into the main airport.

Budget travel is about transparency. The airlines want to hide the total cost until you are three screens deep into the checkout process. If you go into the booking process with these specific line items in mind, you can often find genuine deals. Otherwise, you’re just paying legacy prices for a significantly less comfortable experience.
