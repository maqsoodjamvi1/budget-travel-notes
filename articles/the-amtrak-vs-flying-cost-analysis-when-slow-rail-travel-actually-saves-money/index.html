I used to assume that flying was always the cheapest way to get from point A to point B. If a budget airline popped up with a $59 fare, I booked it instantly. But after a string of frustrating travel days involving $40 airport parking, $70 checked bag fees, and a $15 airport sandwich that tasted like cardboard, I started doing the math differently.

When you look at the total door-to-door cost, taking the train is sometimes the cheaper option. To figure out when slow rail actually saves money, I started tracking every hidden expense of my trips. Here is what I found.

The Hidden Costs of Budget Flights That Rail Eliminates

The base ticket price for a flight is rarely what you actually pay. When you are trying to travel on a tight budget, airlines nickel-and-dime you at every turn.

Take a recent trip I planned from Chicago to Washington, D.C. A budget airline offered a $75 one-way flight. That sounded great until I added up the mandatory extras:

  • Bags: A carry-on bag with wheels cost $35 each way, because ultra-low-cost carriers only include a tiny under-seat personal item.
  • Seat selection: Another $12 to avoid getting randomly assigned a middle seat in the very last row.
  • Ground transport: Getting to O’Hare or Midway airport via rideshare cost me $45, whereas catching the train meant taking a $2.50 city bus to Union Station.

Suddenly, that $75 flight ballooned into $167. Meanwhile, an Amtrak coach ticket on the Cardinal route was $94 flat. Amtrak includes two large personal items and two carry-on bags for free, and my train station was right in the city center. When you add up the baggage fees and airport transit, the train came out significantly cheaper.

Factor in the Cost of a Hotel Night

One of the best-kept secrets of long-distance train travel is that it can act as a two-for-one: transportation and lodging rolled into one.

If you take a flight that departs at 6:00 AM, you have to get to the airport by 4:00 AM. To make that happen, you either need to pay for a night at an airport hotel the evening before, or pay for an expensive pre-dawn rideshare.

With Amtrak, you can book an overnight route—like the Lake Shore Limited from Chicago to New York—and sleep while the miles tick away. If you book a standard coach seat, it is essentially the cost of your travel for the day, and you bypass paying for a hotel room that night. While sleeping upright in coach isn’t for everyone, Amtrak’s seats have generous recline, legrests, and vastly more legroom than any airplane. If you can sleep on a plane, you can definitely sleep on a train.

The Baggage and Food Math

Airports are designed to separate you from your money. Once you clear security, a bottle of water costs $4 and a basic salad costs $15. On a multi-leg journey with layovers, you can easily spend $40 just keeping yourself fed at airport food courts.

Amtrak takes a much more relaxed approach to provisions. You are allowed to pack a cooler or a backpack full of your own groceries, snacks, and even drinks. You can bring a bottle of wine or a six-pack of beer on board to enjoy at your seat (though technically you can only consume alcohol purchased from the cafe car if you are sitting there, plenty of passengers pack their own meals to eat at their seats).

Furthermore, baggage fees on trains simply do not exist in the way they do for airlines. You can bring four pieces of luggage total (two personal items and two carry-on bags) for free. If you are moving across the country, packing for a long vacation, or hauling gear like musical instruments or camping equipment, Amtrak saves you hundreds of dollars in oversized or overweight airline baggage fees.

When Flying Is Still the Better Budget Choice

I am not suggesting you take the train everywhere. Rail travel in the United States has severe limitations, and sometimes flying is still the smarter financial move.

If you are traveling on a short-to-medium distance route where there is no direct rail line—say, traveling from Denver to Seattle—the train will force you to take massive detours, often requiring a bus connection or multi-day layovers. In those scenarios, a direct budget flight will always win on both time and money.

Time is also a form of currency. If a train trip takes 20 hours and a flight takes two, you have to factor in the cost of taking an extra day off work or buying an extra day of meals. If your vacation days are strictly limited, spending three days on a train might cost you more in lost productivity than a slightly more expensive plane ticket.

To decide which option works best for your next trip, map out your door-to-door expenses rather than just looking at the ticket price. Check the cost of getting to the station versus the airport, calculate your baggage needs, and see if a daytime or overnight train fits your schedule. Review your route options to see how the total costs stack up before booking your next getaway.
